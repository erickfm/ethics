Consumer Ethics Within Empire

Consumer complicity calculator

Living inside the United States means participating in systems of exploitation and harm: global supply chains, mass incarceration, data surveillance, stolen land. Nobody opts out entirely. But there's a spectrum between uncritical default and intentional refusal.

This tool maps where your current choices fall on that spectrum and quantifies the harm they produce.

Some options distinguish between choice and constraint. Driving everywhere in rural Appalachia is different from driving everywhere in Brooklyn.

This tool is specifically about consumer ethics within the United States. The structural contexts, data, and resources are US-specific. If you live elsewhere, the frameworks may resonate but the specifics won't apply directly.

How this works & what it's built on


Methodology & Frameworks

What this is

A self-assessment tool that maps your consumer and civic choices across eight dimensions of complicity within the US political economy. It quantifies your annual complicity in life-years of harm, using conversion frameworks from the WHO, EPA, and IPCC.

Scope

This tool covers consumer and civic choices. It does not account for caregiving labor, survival decisions, or circumstances beyond your control. It measures what it measures.

Quantified harm: the life-years model

Each answer you give maps to quantified annual impacts: tonnes of CO₂ emitted, hours of exploited labor purchased, dollars flowing to military and carceral systems. These are converted into a composite unit, "life-years of harm," using four pipelines:

  • Carbon to life-years: Using a conservative Social Cost of Carbon ($120/tonne; Biden EPA finalized $255/tonne in 2023, Trump administration is revisiting) and Value of a Statistical Life Year (~$160K; HHS 2025 central estimate is $604K at 3% discount), each tonne of CO₂ converts to 0.00075 life-years. Consistent with Bressler (2021, Nature Communications): 1 excess death per 4,434 tonnes. The actual harm is likely 2-4x higher than our conservative estimate.
  • Exploited labor to life-years: Based on GBD 2019 occupational injury DALY estimates for high-risk sectors, each hour of labor at exploitative conditions converts to approximately 0.001 life-years.
  • Military spending to life-years: Using Brown University's Costs of War data (940,000+ direct deaths and 4.5-4.7 million total deaths from post-9/11 wars, $8 trillion spent), each dollar converts to 0.000000116 life-years.
  • Carceral spending to life-years: Proportional to the 1.9 million person-years of incarceration the US system produces annually (Sentencing Project; Vera Institute). ICE detention surged 84% to 73,000 in January 2026, with DHS aiming for 100,000 capacity (Prison Policy Initiative, Whole Pie 2026).

These conversions are standard in public health economics. The application to individual consumer behavior involves editorial judgment. The numbers represent structural complicity, not direct causation.

Theoretical foundations

The framing draws on several bodies of scholarship and organizing tradition:

  • The Imperial Mode of Living (Ulrich Brand & Markus Wissen): everyday consumption in the Global North is structurally dependent on exploitation of the Global South. Verso, 2021
  • Against Purity (Alexis Shotwell) argues that complicity is universal and should lead to solidarity and action, not paralysis or purity politics. University of Minnesota Press, 2016
  • Ethical Consumer Research Association, the most comprehensive existing methodology for rating ethical dimensions of consumption, using 19 categories across environment, people, animals, and politics. ethicalconsumer.org
  • Movement Generation's Just Transition Framework, mapping the shift from extractive to regenerative economies. movementgeneration.org
  • Capitalism in the Web of Life (Jason W. Moore) examines how "cheap nature" (cheap labor, food, energy, care) subsidizes Global North consumption. Verso, 2015
  • Carceral Capitalism (Jackie Wang) shows how financial extraction and surveillance function as forms of consumption that target racialized communities. Semiotext(e), 2018

Limitations

  • Individual choices happen within structurally determined systems. Collective action is where structural change comes from. That's why "Solidarity & Collective Action" is a category.
  • Many "ethical" choices cost more money or time. Where possible, answer options distinguish between constraint and choice.
  • This is self-reported, not audited.
  • Not covered: healthcare/pharmaceutical complicity, education and student debt, unpaid digital labor (content creation, data generation, community moderation).

The harm benchmark scale

The benchmark scale plots concrete acts on the same spectrum as your results. Score placements are editorial. The data behind each benchmark is cited.
